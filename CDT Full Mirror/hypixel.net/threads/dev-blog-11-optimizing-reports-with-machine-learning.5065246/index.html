Dev Blog #11: Optimizing Reports with Machine Learning

  • Thread starter inventivetalent
  • Start date Aug 29, 2022

Hi! I'm Haylee, aka inventivetalent.

I joined the Hypixel Admin team about two years ago. I started working mainly on our internal moderation systems, which include all the stuff that happens when you use /report. I've since moved to maintain some of our internal services and backend systems, but I still work on improving and optimizing our reporting process.

It only took me a few weeks of poking around reports to notice recurring patterns in the content our players were reporting frequently, and ever since, I have thought about creating a system that could take care of these reports autonomously or at least reduce the time spent on similar reports.

Keep reading to learn about some of the process that finally turned this idea into reality!


This will get technical at points; you have been warned!

► Getting to know the Frameworks​

After an internal discussion about our reporting system a month ago, I finally felt it was time to get started on this somewhat daunting task. I started looking into various frameworks for machine learning.

While I had tinkered with neural networks in the past, I didn't have much experience in machine learning, known as classification. But after trying out a couple of frameworks, I finally settled on Keras and TensorFlow, the latter probably being the most well-known project in this area.

► Gathering Data​

The idea behind training classification ML models, specifically supervised learning, is to provide the learning algorithm with a lot of data and known labels and train the model to a point where it can predict the labels of new data it has never encountered before. In this case, the data is the content other players have reported, and the label is whether the report was accepted or rejected by a staff member.

Because it was the main topic of the discussion mentioned above, we initially started by exporting a bunch of BuildBattle reports and training classification models based on that data (because why start easy, right?). Unfortunately, that first attempt was not very reliable in production, with only a 40-60% accuracy of correctly classifying whether a report would be accepted or not. While that was slightly disappointing, learning about the intricacies of machine learning, classification, and the frameworks involved worked out exceptionally well as a starting point for this topic. It ultimately paved the way for more useful and effective applications.

Along the way, I also created a service I dubbed Pythia to handle all data exporting and simplify testing the trained models on new, unseen data. It would eventually become the system to run predictions on new reports.

One of those areas where this ended up being way more effective than initially thought was chat reports.

Our players talk a lot in the Hypixel Network chat, with almost 9 Million messages being sent every single day across all of our different chat types (public, private, guild, etc.). And with that also comes a lot of chat reports, so giving our human staff a little artificial assistance in handling the ~3,500 chat reports we receive each day on average seemed like a good next step in this adventure - so let's get a bit more into that!

The exporting process has gone through a couple of iterations since the first attempts, but still involves these steps:
  • Load a bunch of reports, usually from the last month or longer, to have a nice amount of data to train on.
  • Take note if it was accepted or rejected - and if a punishment was issued, include the type of punishment.
  • Load the chat messages included in the report.
  • Clean up the messages - this involves converting the text to lowercase, applying Unicode normalization, and removing excessive punctuation and whitespaces.
  • Write the data to a file.
Exporting all the training data instead of just training the model is primarily speed-related. For one, loading things from the database and modifying them takes a substantial amount of time when processing thousands of entries. Secondly, ML algorithms tend to run much faster on GPUs than CPUs - and since our servers don't have (and don't need) any fancy GPUs, it makes much more sense to just download the exported data and parse it locally to run on a GPU.

► Training the Models​

Given that Tensorflow has a lot of very useful guides and examples, I started by looking at their tutorials for text classification. The first trial was just doing a simple binary classification of whether the report was accepted or not (referred to as “yes” and “no” below).

Here’s a snippet of the output of one of these training sessions:




For a bit of context:
  • An epoch is a training step where TensorFlow looks at the entire dataset and trains on that data.
  • binary_accuracy is the measure of how many predictions the model got right vs. how many it got wrong (binary because we only have two classes to identify).

When training these models, you'll usually split up your training dataset (i.e., the reports) into three different batches:
  • Training - the largest subset and, as the name suggests, the data the model is actually training on
  • Validation - a small subset of the training data, usually about 20%, that the model will use as it's going through each training step (epoch) to make sure it's improving
  • Testing - similar to validation, a separate subset of the available data is used after all the training epochs have been completed to validate the model's accuracy on new data the model has not yet seen.

There’s also a neat glossary for machine learning if you’re curious!

In the above snippet, you’ll notice two different accuracy values: binary_accuracy is the accuracy on the training dataset, and val_binary_accuracy is the accuracy on the validation dataset. As you can see, these gradually increase over time, which means our model is doing well!



Various sources agree that ML models with an accuracy of about 70-90% are good enough in most applications. More importantly, it's realistic, especially in areas like this where there will always be some degree of uncertainty.

One of the first models I created for that task was already insanely accurate when tested on new chat reports. It was already classifying 75% of reports correctly without any extra tweaks - which is really impressive. Still, we can do even better than that!

One nice aspect of the output you get from a machine-learning model is that it doesn’t just spit out yes or no answers but a score value ranging from 0.0 to 1.0. And while that technically does mean it could produce yes/no results, 0.0 being 100% no and 1.0 being 100% yes in our case, that usually doesn’t happen in the real world (if it does, your model probably suffers from overfitting or some other flaw).

So now we can basically use this score as the confidence of our predictions:
  • 0.5 would mean the model has no idea if the answer is yes or no
  • 0.9 indicates it is 80% confident the answer is yes
  • 0.4 would indicate it’s 20% confident the answer is no
(By the way the formula for this is abs(score - 0.5) * 2.0)
So now that we have a confidence value for our predictions, we can set a threshold for different actions we want to take.

And when applying it to the test of how well our model is performing on new data, let’s say it needs a confidence of at least 70%; we can bring the chat classification accuracy up to a whopping 90%! \o/

Without any threshold:


With 70% minimum confidence:


Correct Positive Predictions: actually accepted reports / reports predicted to be accepted (true positives)
Correct Negative Predictions: actually rejected reports / reports predicted to be rejected (true negatives)
Correct Predictions Overall: accepted and rejected combined

To be clear, even a confidence score of 100% (again, that usually never happens anyway) doesn't necessarily mean the model's prediction is correct with absolute certainty. It's just the result of the various calculations the ML model is doing based on the data it was trained on before, and there is always room for error.

As you can see, while we predict many reports correctly without a threshold, there are also quite a few that were incorrectly predicted. Applying the threshold filter still allows for a very nice amount of correctly classified reports while reducing the number of false positives and false negatives.

We can also visualize what our model has learned. Tensorflow specifically provides the Embedding Projector website, which takes our training data and the internal values of the model and renders a really neat point cloud from that:

Apart from looking pretty cool, we can also see that the model has separated most of the words in our training data into two distinct areas!

► Putting it to use​

So with some decent models now available to us, we hooked Pythia into our reporting system.

For a few months now, it has been making predictions on newly created reports and increasing/decreasing the priority of reports where it is confident enough about its predictions.

As the next step, we started temporarily muting players while the reports against them were waiting to be reviewed, simply to pull potential bots and other bad actors out of circulation and prevent them from abusing the network's chat any further until our staff make their final conclusions about reports.

You might recall the mention of a report's punishment being exported above. So eventually, I started making a multi-label classification model which will allow the system to predict not only if reports are likely to be punished but also what the punishment should be so we can automate regular mutes and bans for the reports this system is extremely confident about. This is pretty similar to the simple binary classification, just with the addition of a few extra classes for the model to predict.

For the past couple of weeks, we allowed Pythia to make predictions on a report's punishment and apply that for the most confident predictions.
To be clear, this does not mean we are automating all our reports and punishments. Humans still review the majority of reports, and the same goes for appeals of potential wrongful punishments. This system's main goal is to assist our staff members in making decisions more quickly and reduce their overall workload where possible.

And with the confidence threshold system we explained earlier; we can also apply different actions based on that. So, for example, if a report is predicted to be accepted, but the confidence is only ~40%, we can still bump up the priority of that report in our report queues and show a suggestion to staff members on which punishment might be appropriate. Likewise, if the confidence is 60% or higher, we can issue a temporary mute for the reported player until a human reviews the report. And then, for the really confident predictions, we can start looking at automated regular punishments.

And the combination of both predicting if a report would be punished and, if positive, predicting the punishment turned out to be extremely effective! With about half of our chat reports being handled by Pythia, we've successfully optimized the amount of time our staff have to spend on repetitive reports by
  • Prioritizing reports which have been predicted to result in a punishment.
  • Temporarily preventing players from continuing their offensive behavior: about 30% of reported players (3,500 reports in the last 14 days) have received temporary mutes before being reviewed by a staff member.
  • Automating punishments for certain reports with a high probability of being predicted correctly: ~10%, or 1,500 chat reports in the last 14 days, have been handled automatically.
  • (the remaining ~7,500 punishments have been issued by staff).

► Expanding the scope​

Even while I was messing around with BuildBattle reports in the beginning, I thought about different areas where this general idea of machine learning could be applied to the network.

And we've already started implementing similar systems for other report types. For example, image classification turned out to be really effective in detecting inappropriate skins and is actively adjusting the priority of such reports. BuildBattle reports are also getting some more love to make their predictions more accurate than the initial attempts.

Other types of player reports also have the potential to be integrated into this system, as well as more generic data on the network that's not necessarily related to reports. But we won't get into that here, mainly because we can't confidently say how effective those ideas will be. But keep an eye out; there might be more dev blogs on this topic in the future!

► Closing thoughts​

What started as a very theoretical idea ended up massively improving the efficiency of our reporting process beyond anything I had ever imagined.
I'm sure we will find many other areas where we can put this approach to good use in the future!

I'd also like to mention that this system was never created to replace the humans handling reports. While it has greatly improved the efficiency and speed of processing reports, staff members going through reports Pythia cannot reach a clear conclusion, and giving them their final verdict is still essential in this process.

Appeals will also remain human-managed, which is vital for keeping the automated systems as accurate as possible.

And finally, thank you, the players, for using /report! This entire project would literally be impossible without the human input of submitting all of this data into our systems.


Thank you for reading, and have a nice day!
